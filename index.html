Master Thesis

Modeling the Temporal Persistence of Customer Sentiment from Online Reviews in E-Commerce Sales Forecasting

EDC Paris Business School · Master in Data Science & Business Intelligence · 2025

This thesis is currently under review by my supervisor and pending final approval by EDC Paris Business School. The full document will be shared publicly once the process is complete. Thank you for your understanding.

Research Question

To what extent does the usefulness of customer sentiment from online reviews persist over time when incorporated into sales forecasting models in e-commerce contexts?

Overview

This research adopts a quantitative and hybrid approach combining primary and secondary data to analyse how customer reviews influence purchasing decisions and sales forecasting in e-commerce. The study is structured around three main components.

A primary survey with 174 respondents was designed to capture consumer perceptions on the credibility and temporal relevance of online reviews. The goal was to understand how people weigh reviews differently depending on how recent they are, and whether that perception translates into measurable purchasing behavior.

The transactional analysis is based on the Olist Brazilian e-commerce dataset covering 2016 to 2018, including orders, products and reviews. An ETL pipeline was built to process over 100,000 transactions, consolidating and validating the data before any modelling could begin.

The modelling phase combines NLP and forecasting techniques. Sentiment classification was performed using BERTimbau fine-tuned on 2,700 manually labelled reviews. The forecasting model integrates an Attention-based BiLSTM with XGBoost to capture temporal patterns and correct residual errors. An ablation study was conducted across three model configurations to measure the incremental impact of review metrics and sentiment on predictive accuracy, evaluated through MAE, RMSE and WMAPE across multiple forecasting horizons of 1, 7, 14 and 30 days.

Career

From Industrial Engineer to Data Analyst: my career switch

Ilse Emerich · 2025

When people hear Industrial Engineering and Data Analysis, they often think of two completely different fields. For me, they have always felt closely connected.

During my Industrial Engineering degree, I spent a lot of time studying probability, statistics and process analysis. Those subjects quickly became my favorite part of the program because they helped explain how decisions could be supported by evidence rather than intuition. At the same time, my education was strongly focused on continuous improvement methodologies such as Kaizen, Lean Manufacturing and Six Sigma. I learned how to identify inefficiencies, understand root causes and design better processes.

After graduation, my career naturally moved toward process coordination and process improvement roles. I genuinely enjoyed understanding how organizations operate and finding opportunities to make them work better. Looking back, those experiences gave me a very analytical way of thinking that still helps me today.

A turning point came when I had the opportunity to join Salud Digna as a Data Analyst. Salud Digna is one of the most recognized healthcare organizations in Mexico, providing affordable diagnostic and preventive health services to millions of people every year. Its impact extends beyond Mexico and its model has gained attention throughout Latin America because of the way it combines accessibility, efficiency and quality healthcare.

Working there allowed me to discover the real impact that data can have on decision making. Every project presented new challenges and new opportunities to learn. I had the chance to work with talented professionals who generously shared their knowledge and helped me grow. I remain deeply grateful to my managers, teammates and colleagues, many of whom I still consider friends today.

That experience confirmed something I had been feeling for a long time. I wanted to specialize in data.

Pursuing a master's degree had always been one of my personal goals, but I believed it was important to first understand what truly motivated me professionally. I think that clarity often comes only after entering the workforce and gaining real experience. Once I discovered how much I enjoyed working with data, the decision became much easier.

At the same time, France had always been a personal dream. Moving abroad required courage and a willingness to step outside my comfort zone, but it also represented an opportunity to grow both professionally and personally. Choosing to come to France and pursue my master's degree became one of the most important decisions of my life.

Today, I see Industrial Engineering and Data Analysis as highly complementary disciplines. Industrial Engineering taught me how organizations work, how processes create value and how continuous improvement drives results. Data Analysis provides the tools to measure, understand and improve those processes using evidence.

This combination allows me to bring both business understanding and analytical thinking to a project. I enjoy connecting data with operational reality, translating complex information into actionable insights and identifying opportunities for improvement that create measurable impact.

For that reason, I don't see my career as a transition from one field to another. I see it as the natural evolution of skills that have always been connected.

SQL vs Python for data cleaning: what I've learned

Ilse Emerich · 2025

One of the questions I hear most often from people starting in data is whether SQL or Python is better for data cleaning.

After working with both, my answer is usually the same. It depends on the problem.

When I first started working with data, I tended to think there had to be a single best tool. Over time I realized that the most effective analysts are not the ones who use one tool for everything. They are the ones who know when each tool makes sense.

SQL is often my first choice when the data already lives inside a database. It is fast, efficient and easy to understand. Tasks such as filtering records, removing duplicates, standardizing values or creating aggregated tables can often be done directly in SQL with very clean and readable code.

Python becomes especially valuable when the cleaning process is more complex. Working with multiple files, handling unusual formats, applying custom transformations or automating repetitive tasks can be much easier with libraries such as Pandas.

What surprised me the most was that the real question is rarely SQL or Python. In many projects, the best solution is SQL and Python.

I often use SQL to retrieve and structure the data and then use Python for additional transformations, validation or exploratory analysis. Combining both tools allows me to take advantage of the strengths of each one.

Another lesson I have learned is that data cleaning is not the most glamorous part of analytics, but it is often the most important. A dashboard, report or machine learning model can only be as reliable as the data behind it.

Today I spend less time asking which tool is better and more time asking which tool is more appropriate for the task at hand. That shift in mindset has probably helped me more than learning any specific function or library.

Why the industry never mattered as much as the question behind the data

Ilse Emerich · 2025

There is something I have noticed about myself over the years. No matter what role I was in or what sector I was working in, I always ended up asking the same kind of questions. Why is this happening? What does this number actually mean? What is the story behind this data?

I think that curiosity is what has driven my career more than anything else. Not just the technical side of working with data, but the genuine desire to understand what is going on inside an organization. Every dataset feels like a puzzle to me, and I find it hard to walk away without trying to understand what it is really saying.

My path has gone through very different environments. During my time at Salud Digna, one of the most recognized healthcare organizations in Mexico and Latin America for making quality diagnostic services accessible to millions of people, I worked with operational KPIs on a scale that made the stakes feel very real. The data was not abstract. It represented processes, people and decisions that had a direct impact on how the organization served its patients. That experience taught me that data quality is not a technical concern. It is a responsibility.

Today I work at Air Liquide, a world leader in gases, technologies and services for industry and health, as part of an HR team with strong connections to multiple business areas across an international region. The work involves training budget tracking, provider performance analysis and cross-functional reporting. What I find most interesting about working in HR with data is that people data is some of the most complex and meaningful data you can work with. It combines behavior, investment, organizational strategy and human development all at once. The collaboration across teams also means that the data needs to speak to very different audiences, which is one of my favorite challenges.

What I have realized through all of these experiences is that the industry was never really the point. The questions were. Whether the context is healthcare, operations, or people and learning, the analytical process is the same. You start with curiosity, you look for patterns, you try to understand the why, and then you find a way to communicate what you found so that it actually helps someone make a better decision.

That is what I love about data. It is a way of satisfying a very deep curiosity about how things work. And that curiosity, I think, does not belong to any one industry.
